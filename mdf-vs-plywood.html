Difference Between MDF and Plywood

Both materials are common in woodworking, but they behave very differently in cutting, finishing and daily use.

MDF, or medium density fiberboard, is made from wood fibers mixed with resin and pressed into a dense, smooth sheet. Plywood is made from thin layers of wood veneer glued together with the grain direction alternating between layers. Because of that construction, MDF and plywood feel different, cut different and perform differently in furniture and cabinet work.

MDF is valued for its flat surface. It is excellent for painted doors, wardrobe panels, decorative wall panels, CNC work, speaker boxes and projects where a smooth finish matters. It has no visible grain, so primer and paint can look very clean when the surface is prepared properly. MDF also tends to be consistent across the sheet, which helps when cutting small parts or routing profiles.

The weakness of MDF is that it is heavy and less resistant to moisture. Standard MDF can swell if water reaches the surface or edge. Screws can also strip more easily if they are placed too close to the edge without pilot holes. For cabinets in dry rooms, MDF can be a practical and economical choice. For damp areas, exterior conditions or parts that need high screw strength, plywood is often safer.

Plywood is stronger for its weight because the veneer layers create a cross-grain structure. It is commonly used for cabinet boxes, shelves, drawer parts, flooring underlayment, structural panels, shop furniture and jigs. Good plywood holds screws well and resists bending better than MDF at similar thicknesses. It can also be finished with clear coat if the face veneer is attractive.

Plywood quality varies widely. Cheap plywood may have voids inside, uneven thickness, rough faces or weak glue lines. High quality cabinet plywood costs more but cuts cleaner and stays flatter. MDF is usually more uniform, but it creates fine dust when cut, so dust extraction and a respirator are important.

When choosing between MDF and plywood, think about the final use. For painted cabinet doors, smooth panels and decorative interior work, MDF is often excellent. For strong shelves, screw-heavy joinery, lightweight cabinets or areas with possible moisture, plywood is often better. Many professional projects use both: MDF for painted fronts and plywood or chipboard for the cabinet body.

Edge treatment is another difference. MDF edges are absorbent and usually need sanding, sealing and primer before painting. Plywood edges show layers, which can be attractive in modern furniture or covered with edge banding for a cleaner cabinet look. If the edge will be visible, decide the finish before calculating pieces. A design that uses exposed plywood edges may require higher grade plywood, while painted MDF edges may require extra labor for filling and sealing. These finishing details affect cost just as much as the sheet count.

In a cutting calculator, always separate MDF and plywood as different materials. Even when sheet sizes are the same, the price, weight and cutting requirements are not identical. Grouping them correctly gives a clearer estimate and helps prevent ordering the wrong number of sheets.

Quick choice

Use MDF for smooth painted surfaces. Use plywood when strength, screw holding or lighter weight matters.
