How to Calculate a Kerf Allowance

Every saw cut removes material. Kerf allowance keeps a cutting plan realistic in the workshop.

Kerf is the width of material removed by a saw blade. If a blade cuts a slot that is 3 mm wide, the kerf is 3 mm. This may sound small, but it matters when many pieces must be cut from one sheet. A layout can appear to fit on screen, but if the cuts are planned with no kerf allowance, the final pieces may end up short.

Different tools have different kerf widths. A fine track saw blade may remove less material than a heavy table saw blade. A panel saw, circular saw, jigsaw or CNC bit may all remove different amounts. The correct kerf value is the actual width of the cut made by the tool you will use, not only the thickness printed on the blade.

The easiest way to measure kerf is to cut a scrap piece, then measure the width of the slot with calipers. If calipers are not available, cut a known length into several strips, add the strip widths together and compare the total to the original length. The missing material is the combined kerf from all cuts.

To include kerf in planning, add the kerf width between neighboring pieces or leave a margin around each cut. For example, if four strips are cut side by side and each separation cut removes 3 mm, three internal cuts remove 9 mm total. If both outer edges also need trimming, add those cuts as well. This is why a sheet that appears exactly full is risky: there is no room for the blade.

In practical cabinet work, many carpenters add a small oversized allowance to rough cuts, then trim parts to final size. This is especially useful for visible panels, doors and shelves that must line up perfectly. Rough cutting also makes large sheets easier to handle before final precision work.

Kerf planning becomes more important when repeated parts are cut from one strip. If you need ten identical shelf pieces, do not only multiply the shelf width by ten. Count the cuts between them and add the blade kerf for each cut. If the first edge needs trimming, include that trim cut too. This method may show that an extra strip or a different orientation is needed. It is better to discover that during planning than after the final shelf is a few millimeters short.

Kerf also affects grain matching and laminate sheets. If two pieces must sit next to each other visually, the missing blade width can interrupt the pattern. Plan the cut sequence before you mark the sheet, and always keep the waste side of the blade clear in your mind.

The Wood Sheet Calculator is useful for estimating sheet count and visual layout. Before final cutting, review the output and add your real kerf allowance based on your saw, blade, cutting method and required finish quality.

Rule of thumb

If the layout is extremely tight, buy extra material or redesign the cut plan with kerf included.
